# IT Essentials Study Notes

A summary of 22 exam topics covering software licensing, networking, security compliance, change management, customer service, and programming fundamentals.

---

## 1. Open Source Licenses
**Answer:** Allows users to modify and share the source code.

Open source software gives everyone access to the underlying source code, permitting inspection, modification, and redistribution. This fosters collaborative improvement rather than closed, proprietary development.

## 2. Firewall Port for SSH
**Answer:** TCP port 22.

To securely manage a Linux server remotely, SSH (Secure Shell) is used, which operates by default over TCP port 22. Opening this port on the firewall allows encrypted remote administration.

## 3. Level One Technician Duties
**Answer:** Gathering information when a customer calls for help.

A level one technician is the first point of contact — they listen, identify the issue, and log details into a ticket. Unresolved issues get escalated to higher-level techs.

## 4. Discovering Illegal Content on Company Equipment
**Answer:** Report the findings through the proper channels.

A technician should never personally delete files or confront the user. Instead, follow the company's security policy and report through official channels.

## 5. Boolean Logic — False Condition
**Answer:** `8 != 8`

`!=` means "not equal to." Since 8 equals 8, the statement "8 is not equal to 8" evaluates to **false**. All other comparisons in the question were true.

## 6. HIPAA — PHI and ePHI
**Answer:** PHI and ePHI

- **PHI** (Protected Health Information): any identifiable health data (name, address, medical history).
- **ePHI**: the same information in electronic form (stored/transmitted digitally).

Both apply here since a printed record (PHI) was sent electronically to a printer (ePHI) — governed under HIPAA.

## 7. Personal Software Licenses
**Answer:** A user is allowed to install the software on only one computer.

Personal ("single-user") licenses restrict use to one device per purchase. Copying to other machines or sharing violates the license terms.

## 8. Definition of Cyber Law
**Answer:** The collection of international, country, and local laws that affect computer security professionals.

Cyber law is a broad legal framework — not a single statute — covering data privacy, intellectual property, cybercrime, and e-commerce across jurisdictions.

## 9. Handling Customer Property
**Answer:** A customer's phone list is customer property and must be kept private.

All data on a customer's device (contacts, files, photos) is their private property. Technicians must never access or share it without authorization.

## 10. Definition of a Call Center
**Answer:** A place within a company that provides computer support to both employees and customers.

Call centers aren't only for external clients — they also serve as internal IT support hubs for company staff.

## 11. Authorization to Modify Customer Software
**Answer:** Customer authorization.

Before making any software changes, a technician must get explicit permission from the customer to protect their data and confirm the technician's legitimate authority to act.

## 12. Change Management — Cost & Resources
**Answer:** Assessment.

The **assessment** phase evaluates the feasibility of a proposed change — analyzing cost, required resources/equipment, and risk before implementation.

## 13. Documentation for Data Collection & Retention
**Answer:** Policies.

Organizational **policies** are formal documents defining what data is collected and how long it must be retained, ensuring legal compliance and internal governance.

## 14. Examples of Good Customer Service
**Answer:**
- Notifying the customer promptly about a possible delay.
- Following up with the customer after service completion.

Proactive communication builds trust; follow-up confirms the issue was fully resolved and shows ongoing care.

## 15. Starting a Conversation with a Customer
**Answer:** Learn the customer's name and build rapport, then ask questions to assess their technical knowledge level.

Establishing rapport first makes the customer comfortable; assessing their knowledge level allows the technician to explain solutions at the right complexity.

## 16. Level Two Technician — First Action After Escalation
**Answer:** Call the customer back to ask additional questions and resolve the problem.

Once a case is escalated, the level two tech takes ownership — contacting the customer directly to gather more detail and apply specialized expertise.

## 17. Handling a Talkative Customer
**Answer:** Let the customer finish speaking without interruption, then use closed-ended questions to gather needed data.

Interrupting is unprofessional. After the customer finishes, closed-ended (yes/no) questions help regain control of the conversation efficiently.

## 18. Compiled vs. Interpreted Languages
**Answer:** Java and C#.

Java and C# require a **compiler** to convert source code into a standalone executable before running. Python, PowerShell, and VBScript are **interpreted** — executed line-by-line without prior compilation.

## 19. Handling an Angry Customer — Actions to Avoid
**Answer:**
- Putting the customer on hold or transferring the call.
- Spending time explaining what caused the problem.

Both actions increase frustration — hold/transfer feels dismissive, and lengthy explanations sound like excuses. Listening, empathizing, and apologizing de-escalates the situation instead.

## 20. Secure Remote Access Tool for Linux
**Answer:** SSH client.

SSH encrypts all commands and data sent to a remote Linux machine, protecting against interception. Telnet is insecure (plain text) and unsuitable alternatives don't support cross-platform Linux administration.

## 21. Task Requiring Escalation to Level Two
**Answer:** Changing CMOS settings.

Routine physical tasks (cleaning a mouse, checking cables, replacing toner) are handled at level one. CMOS settings control critical hardware/boot configuration — incorrect changes can prevent booting, so this requires level two expertise.

## 22. Examples of Personally Identifiable Information (PII)
**Answer:** Credit card number and street address.

PII is any data that can identify a specific individual alone or combined with other data. Credit card numbers (financial) and street addresses (location) are classic examples.
