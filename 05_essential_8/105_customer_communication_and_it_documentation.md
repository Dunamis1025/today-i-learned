# IT Professional Skills — Study Summary

## 1. Overview: The Role of an IT Professional
IT professionals regularly handle sensitive customer data and confidential information, whether interacting with clients in person, by phone, or remotely. This makes it essential to:
- Follow strict legal and ethical standards
- Develop strong communication skills (as important as technical repair skills)
- Learn scripting to automate repetitive tasks (e.g., backups, diagnostics) using basics like variables, conditions, and loops

---

## 2. Communication and Troubleshooting

### Why Communication Matters
Fixing computers isn't just a technical skill — communication is critical to correctly identifying the root cause of a problem. Good service requires:
- Setting clear expectations
- Sticking to agreed timelines
- Communicating progress
- Offering repair/replacement options
- Following up to confirm customer satisfaction

### Professional Behavior
- Maintain a professional appearance and tone whether in person or on the phone
- Be mindful of body language in person; smiling can improve tone of voice on calls
- Treat every customer as a "fresh start" — never carry frustration from one call into the next

### The "Know, Relate, Understand" Framework
| Principle | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Know** | Use the customer's name, or the name they prefer |
| **Relate** | Build a one-on-one connection, show empathy, demonstrate you're doing your best to help |
| **Understand** | Match the customer's technical knowledge level — simple terms for beginners, technical terms for experienced users |

## 3. Active Listening & Questioning Techniques

### Active Listening
- Let the customer finish their story without interrupting (interrupting feels disrespectful)
- Use short verbal cues ("I understand," "I see," "Okay") to show engagement
- Summarize what was said to confirm understanding and build trust

### Types of Questions
| Type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Open-ended** | Get detailed explanations/context | "What were you doing when the problem started?" |
| **Closed-ended** | Get quick, specific, factual answers (yes/no or short facts) | "Are you running Windows 10?" |

Information gathered is used to complete an accurate work order.

## 4. Call Scenario Lessons (Good vs. Bad Examples)

### A. Basic Communication (Rodrigo call)
- **Bad:** Interrupting the customer and re-asking things already stated
- **Good:** Confirming preferred name, summarizing the issue back to the customer, and asking permission before proceeding ("Can we walk through the easiest solution first?")

### B. Transferring a Call (Beth/Paul call)
- **Bad:** Transferring without explanation or consent, forcing the customer to repeat their issue
- **Good:**
  - Explain *why* a transfer is needed
  - Brief the new technician beforehand
  - Give the customer the new tech's name/extension in case the call drops
  - Result: seamless handoff, no repeated explanations

### C. Staying Focused / Not Oversharing (Lila call)
- **Bad:** Getting pulled into personal small talk (family stories, recipes) and losing focus on the actual problem
- **Good:** Briefly acknowledge personal comments, then politely steer back to relevant technical questions; verify existing account details efficiently

### D. Handling a Difficult/Rude Customer (Chris call)
- **Bad:** Making sarcastic remarks ("Oh, here we go") about the customer, putting them on hold rudely
- **Good:** Stay calm regardless of the customer's tone, acknowledge their frustration, offer flexible options (wait for a specific tech vs. solve it now) rather than flatly refusing

### E. Handling an Experienced/Technical Customer (Claire call)
- **Bad:** Re-asking for info already on file, misgendering the customer ("sir"), causing loss of trust
- **Good:** Check the file first to avoid repeat questions, confirm the customer's preferred name, summarize the issue accurately before transferring to a Level 2 technician so the customer doesn't repeat themselves

### F. Handling an Emotional Customer (Barbara call)
- **Bad:** Joining in on negative comments about the customer's relatives ("grubby little fingers," "brats") instead of solving the problem
- **Good:** Apologize for wait times, empathize with the customer's frustration (losing access to important work), calmly determine what still works (e.g., "Can you log in?") to find a path forward

### G. Handling a Non-Technical Customer (Peter call)
- **Bad:** Using jargon ("pin," "taskbar") the customer doesn't understand, then condescendingly taking remote control without consent
- **Good:** Explain in plain language, describe what remote-control software does, and let the customer choose whether to be guided step-by-step or hand over control — respecting their autonomy

## 5. Netiquette (Digital Communication Etiquette)
- Always be polite and pleasant
- Start emails with an appropriate greeting (unless continuing an existing thread)
- Never send spam/chain letters
- Never send or respond to "flames" (angry, accusatory messages)
- Avoid ALL CAPS
- Check spelling/grammar before sending
- Maintain ethical standards
- Never write something in an email/text you wouldn't say to someone's face

## 6. Keeping a Call Focused
- Use clear language; avoid jargon the customer won't understand
- Listen actively; use open- and closed-ended questions appropriately
- Give feedback showing understanding while keeping tone friendly and positive
- Stay calm and non-defensive even with difficult customers — don't take criticism personally

## 7. Behaviors to Avoid With Customers
- Don't minimize their problems
- Don't use jargon, abbreviations, or slang
- Don't use a negative tone
- Don't argue or get defensive
- Don't make culturally insensitive remarks
- Don't discuss customer interactions on social media
- Don't be judgmental or insulting
- Don't get distracted or interrupt
- Don't take personal calls during a customer interaction
- Don't chit-chat with coworkers about unrelated topics during a call
- **Avoid unnecessary or abrupt holds** (i.e., don't put someone on hold suddenly/without warning)
- Don't transfer calls without explaining why and getting consent
- Don't criticize other technicians in front of customers
- If running late, notify the customer as soon as possible

## 8. Documentation in IT Organizations

### Purpose of Documentation
- Explains how products/software/hardware work (via diagrams, manuals, knowledgebase articles)
- Standardizes procedures so tasks can be repeated accurately
- Sets rules/restrictions on using company assets (e.g., acceptable use policies)
- Reduces confusion/mistakes, saving time and resources
- Ensures compliance with government/industry regulations
- Trains new employees or customers
- **Must be kept up to date** — IT environments change quickly, so documents need regular scheduled review

### Four Categories of IT Documentation

**1. Policy Documents**
- Acceptable Use Policies (AUP) — rules for using organizational technology
- Security Policies — password rules, incident response methods
- Regulatory Compliance Policies — laws/regulations the organization must follow
- Disaster Recovery Policies — plans for restoring services after an outage

**2. Operation and Planning Documents**
- IT strategy & planning documents (short/long-term goals)
- Project proposals and approvals
- Meeting presentations and minutes
- Budgets and purchasing records (financial/procurement documentation)
- Inventory management (hardware/software, licenses, asset tags, barcodes)

**3. Project Documents**
- User requests for changes/updates/new services
- Software design & functional requirements (flow diagrams, source code)
- Network topology diagrams, equipment specs, device configurations
- Change management forms
- User testing/acceptance forms

**4. User Documentation**
- Descriptions of software/hardware/service features
- End-user manuals
- Help desk ticket database (with resolutions)
- Searchable knowledgebase articles and FAQs

## 9. Key Document/Process Details

### Reports and Procedures
- **AUP (Acceptable Use Policy):** Agreement defining appropriate access to services/resources
- **Incident Report:** Documents major incidents (e.g., security breaches) for root-cause analysis and future prevention
- **Standard Operating Procedures (SOP):** Step-by-step instructions ensuring consistent, efficient task completion
- **Software Installation Procedure:** Check system requirements → validate installation files → confirm licensing → log in change control → update support documentation

### User Checklists
- **New-User Setup:** Onboarding — creating accounts, assigning permissions/devices, security training
- **End-User Termination:** Offboarding — wiping devices, releasing software licenses, removing account access

### Knowledge Base
A **centralized repository** for storing and sharing information org-wide — includes FAQs, troubleshooting guides, internal support databases, training materials, and trustworthy external links, helping users self-solve problems.

### Regulatory Compliance
Organizations must follow federal, state, local, and industry regulations that **dictate** (determine/require) how long data must be retained and how it can be accessed/used. Non-compliance can lead to serious fines or legal consequences.

### Asset Databases
- **Database systems** track hardware/software details (model, serial number, user ID, warranty status) for proper maintenance
- **Asset tags/IDs** (barcodes, QR codes, RFID) work with databases to manage assets from purchase through disposal

## 10. Asset Procurement Lifecycle
| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| **Planning** | Analyze current/future business needs and potential operational impact |
| **Procurement** | Set budget, choose a supplier/vendor |
| **Deployment** | Securely install/integrate the asset |
| **Maintenance** | Apply updates/replacements to keep the asset functional |
| **Disposal** | Wipe all data, then sell, donate, recycle, or destroy the asset |

All hardware assets should have accessible documentation: invoices, warranties, support contracts, and vendor contact info. Assets are assigned to individuals or shared across the organization.

## 11. Change Control Process
Necessary to prevent IT changes from disrupting business operations:

1. **Identification** — What is the change, why is it needed, who are the stakeholders?
2. **Assessment** — Analyze business impact, cost/resources needed, and risks
3. **Planning** — Set timeline, check for downtime, define a rollback/recovery plan
4. **Approval** — Get authorization from proper stakeholders
5. **Implementation** — Notify stakeholders, perform the change, test results
6. **Acceptance** — Define acceptance criteria, designate who approves the outcome
7. **Documentation** — Update change logs and relevant IT documentation to reflect the change

## Key Vocabulary Notes
- **Factual** = fact-based, objective, verifiable (vs. opinion-based)
- **Abrupt** = sudden, without warning (shares root with "dictator" via Latin *dictare*)
- **Procurement** = the full process of acquiring goods/services (broader than "purchasing")
- **Repository** = a centralized storage location (same core concept as a GitHub repo, just for documents/information instead of code)
- **Dictate** = to determine/require/impose (shares Latin root *dictare* with "dictator")
