# IT Support & Technician Communication — Study Notes

A summary of key concepts covering professional/ethical technician communication practices and basic programming fundamentals.

---

## 1. Ethical Communication with Customers

- **Rule:** A technician must only send **solicited emails** (emails the customer requested or agreed to receive).
- **Why:** Ethical communication relies on **consent and trust**. Unsolicited mass emails, forged emails, and chain emails are considered spam — they violate customer trust and pose security risks.

## 2. Professionalism During Troubleshooting Calls

- **Rule:** A technician must **maintain professional behavior at all times**.
- **Why:** Professionalism (a calm, respectful, objective demeanor) is the overarching standard for customer service. Asking personal questions is inappropriate; automatically escalating every issue without attempting resolution is poor practice.

## 3. Signs of Professional Communication

- **Correct behaviors:**
  - Clarifying what the customer said *after* they finish explaining.
  - Active listening — using short verbal cues like "I see" / "I understand."
- **Incorrect behaviors:** Interrupting, forcing the customer to paraphrase themselves, or focusing only on what can't be done.

## 4. Call Prioritization

- **Highest priority:** A **total system failure** that stops the company from operating — causes the greatest business/financial impact, so it needs an immediate response.
- **Lower priority:** RAM upgrades, feature requests, isolated computer errors, or limited network access issues.

## 5. Handling an Angry Customer Who Wants a Specific (Unavailable) Technician

- **Best approach:** Offer to help immediately yourself, and give a clear time frame (e.g., "the technician will call back within 2 hours") if they'd rather wait.
- **Why:** This shows initiative and sets **clear expectations**, reducing frustration.
- **Avoid:** Immediately escalating to a supervisor, ignoring the request, or forcing an unwanted path — these can **exacerbate agitation**.

## 6. Handling a Stressed Customer

- **Best approach:** Try to **establish rapport** — build a connection through empathy, active listening, and a calm demeanor.
- **Why:** Once a customer feels heard, they're more likely to cooperate with troubleshooting.
- **Avoid:** Transferring the customer, telling them to call back later, or leaving them on hold for an **arbitrary** (unexplained) amount of time.

## 7. What Counts as Rude/Disrespectful Behavior

- **Cause of a rudeness complaint:** Interrupting the customer multiple times to ask questions.
- Other actions (standard procedures, skipped small talk) are not typically seen as disrespectful.

## 8. Closed-Ended vs. Open-Ended Questions

- **Closed-ended question example:** "Is this the first time the error occurred?" (answerable with yes/no or a short fact)
- **Open-ended questions** require detailed explanations (e.g., "What happened?", "What message appeared?").

## 9. Who Uses Remote Access Software

- **Correct answer (per course material): Level two technicians** use remote access software to update customer computers.
  - *(Note: this was corrected — level one technicians handle first contact, but remote software use is attributed to level two.)*
- **Bench technicians:** Perform repairs/warranty work at a central depot.
- **Field technicians:** Travel to the customer's location (home, business, school) to work on-site.

## 10. Backing Up Customer Data

- **Rule:** A technician may only back up personal/confidential data **if the customer permits it**.
- **Why:** Consent is essential — handling private data without permission risks privacy violations and security issues.

## 11. Documenting Suspected Illegal Activity

When illegal activity is suspected on a customer's computer, immediately document:
1. **Why** the technician accessed the computer (justification).
2. **Evidence** of the suspected illegal activity.
3. **Location** of the computer.

## 12. Handling a Talkative Customer

- **Avoid:** Asking social/small-talk questions like "How are you today?" — this encourages more off-topic talk.
- **Recommended:** Use closed-ended questions or politely redirect the conversation back to the technical issue.

## 13. Core Responsibilities of Level One Technicians

- **Escalating** trouble tickets to level two technicians when issues are complex or **persistent**.
- **Gathering customer information** to create/initiate a work order.
- (They serve as the **first point of contact** for support.)

## 14. What Is a Call Center?

- **Definition:** A place that provides **computer support to customers**, typically remotely (phone/online) — not a repair shop or appointment-booking service.

## 15. When a Technician Identifies the Problem Before the Customer Finishes Explaining

- **Correct action:** Wait politely for the customer to finish explaining.
- **Why:** Interrupting is disrespectful, and the customer might mention additional important details.

## Programming Basics

## 16. Data Type for Currency with Decimals

- **Correct type: `float`** (floating-point) — represents numbers with decimal points, needed for financial values like account balances.
- **Incorrect types:**
  - `bool` → true/false only
  - `char` → a single character
  - `int` → whole numbers only (no decimals/**fractional** values)

## 17. Comparison Operators

- Given `A = 5`, `B = 7`, which expression evaluates to **true**?
  - **`A != B`** → true, since 5 is not equal to 7.
  - `A > B`, `A >= B`, `A == B` → all false.

## 18. Interpreted vs. Compiled Languages

- **Interpreted language (executes line-by-line at runtime): `PowerShell`**
- **Compiled languages (translated to machine code before running):** C++, Java, C#

## Key Vocabulary Covered

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Consent | Voluntary, informed agreement — free from coercion or deception |
| Demeanor | A person's outward behavior/manner/conduct |
| Necessitate | To make something necessary or required |
| Transparently | Openly and honestly, without hiding information |
| Exacerbate | To make a bad situation worse |
| Agitation | A state of distress, anxiety, or being upset |
| Rapport | A close, trusting relationship between two people |
| Arbitrary | Random or without a clear justification |
| Excessively | To an extreme or unnecessary degree |
| Persistent | Continuing over time without being resolved |
| Fractional | Involving decimal/non-whole number values |
