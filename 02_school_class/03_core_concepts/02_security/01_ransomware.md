# Ransomware

## What is Ransomware?

Ransomware is a type of malware that blocks access to files or systems and demands money from the victim.

The word **ransom** means money demanded in exchange for returning something.
In ransomware attacks, criminals usually encrypt files and then ask for payment, often in cryptocurrency such as Bitcoin.

In simple words:

**Ransomware = malicious software that locks your data and demands payment**

---

## How Ransomware Works

A ransomware attack usually follows this process:

1. The victim opens a malicious link, email attachment, or infected file.
2. The ransomware installs itself on the device.
3. It encrypts files so the user cannot open them.
4. A ransom note appears on the screen.
5. The attacker demands payment to provide a decryption key.

Sometimes the attacker also steals data before encrypting it.
This is called **double extortion**.

---

## Common Infection Methods

Ransomware can enter a system in many ways, such as:

- Phishing emails
- Malicious attachments
- Fake software downloads
- Unpatched vulnerabilities
- Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) attacks
- Infected websites or drive-by downloads

The most common method is phishing.
A user clicks something that looks normal, and the malware starts running.

---

## What Happens After Infection?

When ransomware infects a device, it may:

- Encrypt personal files
- Lock business documents
- Stop access to important systems
- Spread across the network
- Display a ransom message
- Threaten to leak stolen data

As a result, users may lose access to documents, photos, databases, or even entire business operations.

---

## Why Ransomware Is Dangerous

Ransomware is dangerous because it can cause:

- Data loss
- Financial loss
- Business downtime
- Reputation damage
- Legal and compliance issues
- Stress and panic for victims

Even if the victim pays, there is no guarantee the files will be recovered.

---

## Double Extortion

Modern ransomware often uses **double extortion**.

This means the attacker:

1. steals the data first
2. encrypts the data after that
3. threatens to leak the data if payment is not made

So the victim is pressured in two ways:
- “Pay to unlock your files”
- “Pay or your private data will be exposed”

---

## Simple Example

Imagine a student keeps assignments, photos, and personal files on a laptop.

One day, the student opens a fake email attachment.
After that:

- the files cannot be opened
- the screen shows a warning message
- the attacker demands Bitcoin

This is a ransomware attack.

---

## Signs of a Ransomware Attack

Some common signs are:

- Files suddenly cannot be opened
- File extensions change
- A ransom note appears
- The computer becomes abnormal or slow
- Shared network files are also affected

---

## How to Prevent Ransomware

You can reduce the risk by:

- Not opening suspicious emails or attachments
- Keeping software and operating systems updated
- Using antivirus or endpoint protection
- Backing up files regularly
- Using strong passwords and MFA
- Limiting user permissions
- Disabling unnecessary remote access
- Training users to spot phishing attempts

The most important protection is having **safe offline or cloud backups**.

---

## Should Victims Pay the Ransom?

In general, paying is not recommended because:

- there is no guarantee of recovery
- attackers may ask for more money
- payment encourages more crime

Instead, organisations should follow incident response procedures, isolate infected systems, and report the attack.

---

## Key Terms

- **Malware**: malicious software
- **Encryption**: converting data into unreadable form
- **Decryption key**: a key used to unlock encrypted files
- **Phishing**: tricking users into clicking malicious links or files
- **Bitcoin / cryptocurrency**: digital money often used in ransom demands
- **Double extortion**: stealing data and encrypting it at the same time

---

## Quick Summary

Ransomware is malware that locks or encrypts files and demands payment.
It often spreads through phishing emails, malicious downloads, or security weaknesses.
Modern ransomware may also steal data before encryption.
The best defence is prevention, backups, updates, and user awareness.
