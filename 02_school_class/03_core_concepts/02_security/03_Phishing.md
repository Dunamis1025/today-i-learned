# Phishing

## What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyber attack that tricks people into giving away sensitive information.

The attacker pretends to be a trusted person, company, or website.  
Their goal is to make the victim do something without thinking carefully.

For example, they may try to steal:

- usernames
- passwords
- bank details
- credit card numbers
- verification codes
- personal information

Phishing is one of the most common cyber attacks because it does not always need advanced hacking skills.  
Instead of attacking the computer system directly, the attacker attacks the human mind.

---

## Why is it called "Phishing"?

The word **phishing** sounds like **fishing**.

A fisherman throws bait into the water to catch fish.  
In the same way, a cyber attacker sends a fake message as bait to catch victims.

The bait may look real, urgent, or important, so people click without checking properly.

---

## How Phishing Works

Phishing usually follows a simple process:

1. The attacker creates a fake message.
2. The message looks like it comes from a trusted source.
3. The victim receives the message by email, text, social media, phone call, or fake website.
4. The victim is asked to click a link, open an attachment, send money, or enter personal information.
5. The attacker steals the information or gains access to the account or system.

Phishing works because it uses **social engineering**.  
This means the attacker manipulates human emotions such as fear, urgency, curiosity, trust, or greed.

---

## Common Signs of Phishing

There are many warning signs that a message may be phishing.

### 1. Urgent language
The message tries to rush the victim.

Examples:

- "Your account will be locked today"
- "Immediate action required"
- "Verify your details now"

The attacker wants the victim to panic and act quickly.

### 2. Suspicious sender
The email address or phone number looks strange or slightly different from the real one.

For example:

- support@paypa1.com instead of support@paypal.com
- a random mobile number pretending to be a bank

### 3. Strange links
A link may look safe at first, but the real destination is different.

For example, the message says "Click here to update your account", but the link opens a fake website.

### 4. Poor spelling or grammar
Some phishing messages contain awkward English, spelling mistakes, or unusual sentences.

However, not all phishing messages are badly written.  
Some are very professional.

### 5. Request for sensitive information
Legitimate companies usually do not ask for passwords, banking PINs, or verification codes through email or text.

### 6. Unexpected attachments
The message may include an attachment that installs malware when opened.

Common examples include fake invoices, fake delivery notices, or fake job documents.

## Common Types of Phishing

## 1. Email Phishing

This is the most common type.

The attacker sends a fake email pretending to be from:

- a bank
- a delivery company
- a government agency
- Microsoft, Google, Apple, or PayPal
- a workplace manager or school

The email usually asks the victim to click a link or download an attachment.

---

## 2. Spear Phishing

Spear phishing is more targeted.

The attacker chooses a specific person or organisation and creates a message based on real information.

For example, the attacker may know the victim's:

- name
- job title
- company
- manager's name
- recent activity

Because the message feels personal, it is more convincing.

---

## 3. Whaling

Whaling is phishing aimed at high-level targets such as:

- CEOs
- managers
- company directors
- finance staff

These attacks often try to steal money, sensitive business data, or login details.

---

## 4. Smishing

Smishing means phishing by SMS or text message.

Examples:

- fake parcel delivery message
- fake bank security alert
- fake toll payment message

The victim clicks the link in the text and is taken to a fake website.

---

## 5. Vishing

Vishing means phishing by voice call.

The attacker calls the victim and pretends to be from a trusted organisation such as a bank, government office, or technical support team.

They may ask for:

- account details
- passwords
- one-time passcodes
- remote access to the computer

---

## 6. Clone Phishing

In clone phishing, the attacker copies a real email and changes the link or attachment into a malicious one.

Because the email looks familiar, the victim may trust it.

---

## What Attackers Want from Phishing

Phishing is used for many different goals.

### Stealing login details
Attackers want usernames and passwords to access email, banking, social media, or work systems.

### Stealing money
They may trick victims into transferring money or entering credit card details.

### Identity theft
They may collect personal information to pretend to be the victim.

### Installing malware
A phishing email may deliver malware such as ransomware, spyware, or keyloggers.

### Business compromise
Attackers may target employees to gain access to company systems and confidential information.

---

## Real Examples of Phishing Messages

Here are simple examples of phishing-style messages:

### Example 1
"Your bank account has been suspended. Click here immediately to verify your identity."

### Example 2
"We tried to deliver your parcel, but the address is incomplete. Pay the small redelivery fee here."

### Example 3
"Your Microsoft password expires today. Log in now to keep your account active."

These messages often create fear or urgency so the victim reacts quickly.

---

## Why Phishing is Dangerous

Phishing is dangerous because one small mistake can lead to a big problem.

A victim may lose:

- money
- personal data
- account access
- company information
- privacy

In organisations, one phishing email can lead to:

- malware infection
- ransomware outbreak
- data breach
- financial loss
- reputation damage

This is why phishing is a major cyber security threat.

---

## How to Prevent Phishing

There is no perfect way to stop all phishing, but good habits can reduce the risk.

### 1. Check the sender carefully
Look at the full email address, not just the display name.

### 2. Do not click links in suspicious messages
Instead, visit the official website directly by typing the address yourself.

### 3. Do not open unexpected attachments
If you were not expecting the file, be careful.

### 4. Think before acting
Phishing often uses urgency.  
Pause and check before you respond.

### 5. Use multi-factor authentication (MFA)
Even if a password is stolen, MFA adds another layer of protection.

### 6. Keep software updated
Updates help protect against malware and other security problems.

### 7. Report suspicious messages
In a workplace, report phishing emails to the IT or security team.

### 8. Learn common phishing patterns
Awareness is one of the best defences.

---

## How to Identify a Fake Website

A phishing website may look very similar to a real one.

Check for:

- strange spelling in the web address
- extra letters or numbers in the domain name
- unusual design or poor layout
- login page that appears after clicking a suspicious link
- missing security details or unusual browser warnings

A fake website may copy logos, colours, and layout, but the domain is often different.

---

## Phishing and Social Engineering

Phishing is a type of social engineering.

Social engineering means tricking people into making mistakes.

Instead of breaking into a system with pure technical skill, the attacker manipulates human behaviour.

Common emotions used in phishing:

- fear
- urgency
- curiosity
- greed
- trust
- respect for authority

For example, a message from a "manager" asking for urgent payment may pressure an employee to act without checking.

---

## Simple Summary

Phishing is a cyber attack that tricks people into giving away information or performing unsafe actions.

It often happens through:

- email
- text messages
- phone calls
- fake websites
- social media messages

The attacker pretends to be trustworthy and uses social engineering to fool the victim.

The best protection is to stay calm, check carefully, and never trust unexpected messages too quickly.

---

## Key Points to Remember

- Phishing targets people, not just computers.
- It often uses fake messages and fake websites.
- The goal is usually to steal information, money, or access.
- Common types include email phishing, spear phishing, smishing, and vishing.
- Warning signs include urgency, strange senders, suspicious links, and requests for sensitive information.
- Good awareness and careful checking are the best defences.

---

## One-Line Definition

Phishing is a cyber attack that tricks people into revealing sensitive information or taking unsafe actions by pretending to be a trusted source.
